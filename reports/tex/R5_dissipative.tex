\begin{abstract}
We extend the exact pipeline to the 240 dissipative quantum cellular automata
(rules containing a reset channel $\rt D$ or $\rt E$). The one-cycle channel is
now genuinely non-unitary: the directed transition graph has \emph{transient}
strongly connected components flowing into \emph{recurrent classes}
(attractors). We sweep all dissipative rules (both boundary conventions,
$N=6\ldots13$), census their attractor structure, and classify each recurrent
class by the peripheral spectrum of its exact restricted superoperator (Tier 1b)
as a classical pointer state (\emph{mixing}), a \emph{limit cycle}, or a
\emph{coherence-carrying} attractor. Finally we quantify, exactly at small $N$,
the \emph{protected-coherence gap} --- the amount by which the full channel's
fixed-point space exceeds the classical pointer count --- which the transition
graph provably under-resolves for weakly dissipative rules.
\end{abstract}

\section{Dissipative graph structure}
For a dissipative rule the channel $\Phi_C(\rho)=\sum_\mu K_\mu\rho K_\mu^\dagger$
has $\ge 2$ Kraus operators. The exact engine propagates every Kraus branch
(never merging incoherent alternatives) and records the directed transition
graph $M_{yx}=\sum_\mu|\langle y|K_\mu|x\rangle|^2$. Its terminal strongly
connected components are the recurrent classes (attractors); non-terminal
components are transient. We record, per $(\text{rule},N,\text{bc})$: the number
and sizes of recurrent classes, the transient depth (longest condensation-DAG
path), and the basin structure (states reaching a single vs.\ several
attractors). The engine and its dissipative branch machinery are validated
against an independent dense-channel oracle (528/528 support checks; per-site
Kraus completeness $A_0^\dagger A_0+A_1^\dagger A_1=\mathbb{1}$).

\section{Tier 1b: attractor types}

$\Phi_R$ on $\mathrm{span}(R)\otimes\mathrm{span}(R)^*$ from the labelled Kraus
branches, and read its peripheral spectrum (geometric multiplicities, robust to
the non-normal transient Jordan structure). A class is \emph{mixing} (a unique
steady state; a classical pointer/dark state when $|R|=1$), a \emph{limit cycle}

\section{Strong vs.\ weak symmetry: what each stage can see}
\label{sec:strongweak}
A \emph{strong} symmetry commutes with every Kraus operator individually; a
\emph{weak} symmetry only makes the channel covariant (context \S5). For unitary
rules there is a single Kraus operator and the two coincide. This pipeline does
\textbf{not} detect symmetry \emph{operators} (no commutant of $\{K_\mu\}$, no
charge null-space --- that is Tier 2); it separates the two \emph{operationally},
because the two stages of the pipeline are sensitive to different things:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Tier 1a (graph).} The graph encodes
  $M_{yx}=\sum_\mu|\langle y|K_\mu|x\rangle|^2$, i.e.\ the chain obtained by
  \emph{dephasing in the computational basis after every cycle}. Only
  basis-diagonal \emph{strong} charges fragment it; their signature is the number
  of recurrent classes (multistability, Table~\ref{tab:census}). This is the only
  symmetry information the graph carries.
  \item \textbf{Tier 1b (channel).} The exact Cesaro rank
  $\dim\mathrm{Fix}(\Phi)$ (geometric multiplicity of eigenvalue 1 of the full
  $4^N$ superoperator) sees the coherent fixed-point algebra. Weak symmetries and
  protected coherences appear here and \emph{only} here.
\end{itemize}
The gap $\dim\mathrm{Fix}(\Phi)-\sum_R\dim\mathrm{Fix}(\Phi_R)\ge 0$ measures what
the graph misses (Table~\ref{tab:cesaro}).

\subsection{Caveat: the graph is the dephased chain, and can misplace attractors}
$\Phi$'s populations are \emph{not} determined by $M$ --- coherences feed back into
populations --- so ``transient for $M$'' does not imply ``transient for $\Phi$''.
We therefore test faithfulness exactly (Table~\ref{tab:faithful}) by asking
whether $\mathrm{Fix}(\Phi)$ is contained in the block spanned by the graph's
recurrent states, and whether $\Phi^{t}(\mathbb{1}/d)$ actually flows onto them.
Two regimes occur, and the distinction matters:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Graph-faithful} (202 of 240 rules at $N=4$, \rt{pbc}): e.g.\ rule
  200 --- $\mathrm{Fix}(\Phi)$ lies entirely in the recurrent block and
  $\Phi^{400}(\mathbb{1}/d)$ converges exactly onto the ten pointer states. Here
  ``recurrent class $=$ attractor'' is legitimate and the gap ($100$ vs.\ $10$) is
  genuine \emph{cross-class} coherence: a $10$-dimensional decoherence-free
  subspace built on the graph's own pointer states.
  \item \textbf{Graph-unfaithful} (38 of 240): e.g.\ rule 22 --- fixed-point
  operators carry weight $0.49$ \emph{outside} the recurrent block and
  $\Phi^{400}(\mathbb{1}/d)$ retains $12.5\%$ of its population on
  graph-``transient'' states (limit support: 11 basis states, not 3). Since
  $\dim\mathrm{Fix}=25=5^2$, the true attractor is a \textbf{5-dimensional
  decoherence-free subspace}; the ``three point attractors'' of the graph is an
  artifact of dephasing. Rule 50 ($\dim\mathrm{Fix}=13$, $25\%$ leaked) and rule
  106 ($42\%$) are stronger cases.
\end{itemize}
For unfaithful rules the graph-level attractor language of \S\ref{sec:census}
describes the \emph{dephased} dynamics only; the coherent attractor requires
Tier 1b. This is the quantitative form of the context's warning that the graph
under-resolves dissipative attractor structure.
